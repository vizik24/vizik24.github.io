Climbing Weather Index

ArcGIS Desktop, Python, Excel / Personal Project

Much like most British people, rock climbers love to complain about the weather, especially if it means we can't finish a route. Factors like precipitation, humidity and air temperature have a huge effect on how the rock feels, and how much friction there is between your skin and the rock. For this reason many people only climb Gritstone in the early spring, late autumn and winter. Characterized by smooth faces, slopey holds and the potential for hitting the ground when you fall off, friction can be a huge factor in whether you are able to send a route or not.
The map above shows the average daytime Climbing Weather Index (CWI) for 03/07/2023. Lighter colours represent lower values, and therefore better climbing conditions.

The climbing weather index was calculated using multiple regression analysis to determine the influence of various historical meteorological data on the number of climbers attending three case study crags across the UK. Crags were chosen for 3 popular rock types climbed in the UK (gritstone, rhyolite and limestone). See the equation below, where T = air temperature, H = relative humidity and W = wind speed.

\(CWI = \sqrt{(0.112883T)^2 + (-0.192465H)^2 + (-0.100107W)^2}\)

The CWI was developed using the following data: air temperature, relative humidity, and wind speed. Rain, while it would affect friction greatly, is not included in the CWI as climbing on wet rock leads to increased erosion. Future work could improve upon the CWI by expanding the case study sample data to include data on more weather factors that cover a longer time window, as well as increasing the number of and variation of rock types and climbing styles present in the case study data. It would also be useful for future applications to include a sub-index for the various climbing styles, as different conditions are favourable for bouldering compared to mountaineering, for example.
